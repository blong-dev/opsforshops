# Ops for Shops — Sales Partner Guide

## What We Do (30-Second Version)

Small businesses run on 5-10 software tools that don't talk to each other. Their numbers don't match across systems, they're entering the same data twice, and they're overpaying vendors for features they don't use. I fix that.

**I'm a CTO in a box.** Enterprise architecture background, aimed at protecting independent businesses. I work with whatever they already have — or want to have. No pushing platforms, no vendor loyalties. Just making their operations actually work.

## The Three Services

### 1. Vendor Alignment
Their POS, CRM, accounting, scheduling — none of it syncs. I review what they're paying for, cut what's dead weight, and make the rest line up.

- POS, CRM, and accounting that actually sync
- Find critical services they're overpaying for
- Get the most out of what they already have

### 2. Data Cleanup & Migration
Their reports don't match reality. Inventory says one thing, books say another. Or they're stuck on an old platform and terrified of switching. I find where the numbers go wrong, fix the source, and handle migrations without losing what matters.

- Books that match across every platform
- Safe data migrations between systems (old platform → new, no data loss)
- Numbers they can actually trust when it counts

### 3. Automation
If they're entering the same data twice, something's broken. I build workflows that handle the repetitive stuff — invoices, follow-ups, data entry — so they get hours back in their week.

- Invoices, follow-ups, and data entry on autopilot
- Tools that update each other without a human in the middle
- Time back for the work that actually makes them money

## Who To Target

**Best prospects:**

1. **Office managers / practice admins** — law firms, accounting practices, medical offices, real estate. They're holding everything together with duct tape and drowning in admin busywork. They can justify the spend as an operational expense.

2. **Owners who accidentally became their own IT department** — solo practitioners, small firms (1-15 people). Started the business to do the work, now they spend half their time wrestling software. They have decision authority and they're not cheap — they're protective.

3. **Anyone mid-vendor-migration** — marketing person or office manager who's been handed a platform migration and hit a wall. Budget is already approved for the project — they just need someone who can actually do it.

**The universal tell:** Anyone who says "I should really get my systems sorted out but I don't have time" or "my numbers never match."

**Best verticals:** Trades (HVAC, plumbing, electrical), professional services (law, accounting, medical), retail shops, restaurants, agencies. Anyone running 3+ software tools that don't sync.

**Conversation starters by vertical:**

- **Trades:** "Does your dispatch software talk to your books? Or is someone entering jobs in two places?"
- **Professional services:** "How long does it take your office manager to close out the month? If it's more than a day, something's broken."
- **Retail/restaurants:** "When your POS says one thing and your inventory says another, who figures out which one's right?"
- **Agencies:** "How many hours a week does your team spend on reporting that could be automatic?"

## Typical Client Journey

Most clients follow the same path. One referral turns into three paydays.

**1. Tune-Up** → They pay $500, we sit down for 90 minutes, they get a report with specific fixes. This is where trust gets built. Most people have never had someone look at their whole stack at once.

**2. Project** → The report surfaces 2-3 things that are costing them real time or money. They pick one — usually vendor alignment or a migration they've been putting off. Typical project runs 2-6 weeks, $2,000-$10,000.

**3. Retainer** → After the project, they realize they don't want to manage this stuff themselves. They stay on for monthly ops management — keeping integrations healthy, handling vendor changes, updating automations when the business shifts.

Not every client goes all three steps. Some stop after the Tune-Up with a report they can act on themselves. That's fine — they still refer others. But the ones who go the distance are where the real value compounds, for them and for you.

## Objection Handling

**"I already have an IT guy."**
→ IT guys keep servers running and fix printers. I make your business software work together. Different job entirely. Most of my clients have IT support already.

**"We looked at [Square/Toast/Jobber/HouseCallPro] and they do everything."**
→ All-in-one platforms overpromise. They lock you into their ecosystem, long contracts, and make it painful to leave. I work with what fits YOUR business — not what fits one vendor's revenue model. And if that all-in-one is actually the right call, I'll tell you that too.

**"I don't have the budget right now."**
→ The Tune-Up is $500 and it credits toward future work. Most clients find it pays for itself in the first week — overpaid vendors, duplicate subscriptions, wasted hours. The report alone is worth it even if you never hire us.

**"How is this different from a consultant?"**
→ Most consultants give you a PowerPoint and leave. I do the work. I build it, I own it, I make sure it actually runs. And everything I build, you own — no lock-in to me either.

**"Can you work with [specific software]?"**
→ Yes. I work with whatever you have. POS systems, CRMs, QuickBooks, Xero, scheduling tools, inventory — doesn't matter. That's the point. I'm not selling you a platform. I'm making your existing tools work.
